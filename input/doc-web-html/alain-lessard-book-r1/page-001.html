COAT OF ARMS REPORT

The Coat of Arms was completed by a heraldic artist, from information that was researched in:

Riestaps Armorial General - Normandy, France.

The Coat of Arms (shield) is described heraldically as:

Argent, three martlets gules, on a chief azure, three mullets or...

Martlets are short legged birds of fast movement. Believed to have been a symbol of a religious man who went to the Holy Lands.

Mullets - Star shaped, said to represent rowels of a spur.

The crest is the small decoration which appears above the shield and helmet:

None recorded.

Family mottoes may refer to family exploits or past family history, others refer to the shield, crest or badge device and others are a play upon the name of the holders, started in the distant past as battle cries. The motto for this name is:

None recorded.

History of ALAIN Name

ALLAIN - variation of the name D’ALAIN
ALAIN - first name and family name.

The following definitions are given:

1. The name of many saints; Latin “Alanus”
2. Also, the name of an ethnic people - from the south of Russia in ancient times.

(The above research was carried out by Jean-nine Alain in France.)

Further definitions:

3. In Father Lebel’s story on Simon ALLAIN he says, “ALLAIN signifie d’un blanc harmonieux comme le blanc onctueux de certaines faiances authentiques de Rouen.” [Allain means of a smooth white, such as that of authentic pottery from Rouen (a region in France known for its excellent pottery, among other things).]

The name ALLAIN has a number of meanings. According to the book, The Origin of English Surnames by P.H. Reaney, Routledge and Kegan Paul, London 1967, the French word ALLAIN is the name of a Welsh and Breton saint.

Surnames, generally, can be divided into four categories:

1. Place Names which would denote the location from which a person’s name came.

2. Occupational Names which pertain to office, rank or trade.

3. Descriptive Names which describe physical characteristics, or relationships, such as condition in life appearance, dress and colouring.

4. Kinship Names which are derived from the name of the name of the father. The patronymical surname is common in many forms to all countries, thus in England the son of John became Johnson.
